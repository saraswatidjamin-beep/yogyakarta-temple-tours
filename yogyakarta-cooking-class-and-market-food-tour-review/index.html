Yogyakarta Cooking Class and Market Tour: Honest Review & Tips

By Rama Kusuma, Rama Kusuma is a Javanese temple guide based in Sleman, Yogyakarta

I Didn't Expect Yogyakarta, Indonesia to Feel Like This

I have spent twenty years walking the same stones at Borobudur and Prambanan, guiding travellers through the Kedu Plain and the slopes of Merapi. I know the light on the stupas at dawn and the way the Ramayana ballet sounds when the gamelan echoes off the Prambanan walls at night. But for all those years, the moment that taught me the most about my own city happened in a kitchen, not a templ.

It was 2022, and a French guest had asked me to take her somewhere real after a morning at the Kraton. She was tired of temples. She wanted to taste the city. So I drove her south to Imogiri, to a sate klathak stall where the goat satay is grilled on actual bicycle spokes. The old seller explained that the metal carries heat into the centre of the meat so it cooks evenly with just salt. She had eaten satay all over Asia, she said, and this was the first time anyone showed her why a thing is made the way it is. We stayed two hours. Her quick lunch became the story of her trip.

That afternoon changed how I think about guiding. The temples are the reason people come to Yogyakarta. But the food is the reason they remember it. The cooking class and market tour I finally took myself, years after that lunch, is the closest thing I have found to bottling that Imogiri feeling and handing it to a traveller in a single morning.

Yogyakarta Cooking Class and Market Tour: The Tour That Saved My Trip

I booked the Yogyakarta Cooking Class and Market Tour on a rainy Tuesday in the wet season, partly out of professional curiosity and partly because I had a free morning and wanted to see if the hype was real. The tour starts at the Beringharjo market, the old market in the heart of the city, where your guide walks you through the spice stalls and the produce section and explains what you are actually looking at. Not just the names of the ingredients, but how they are used in Javanese ceremony: the turmeric that colours the tumpeng rice, the salam leaves that go into the slow-cooked chicken, the palm sugar that balances the chilli in the sambal.

The market itself is chaotic, wet, and genuine. You will step over puddles and past women carrying baskets on their heads. Your guide will help you negotiate for a bunch of kemangi (lemon basil) and a chunk of fresh kencur (aromatic ginger). This is not a sanitised experience. It is the real Beringharjo, the one where my mother shopped when I was a child, and I was grateful the tour did not try to polish it.

From the market you drive to a cooking space in a quiet village on the outskirts of the city. The class I took focused on tumpeng, the ceremonial yellow cone of rice that Javanese families serve at life events: births, weddings, circumcisions. The cone is the mountain, the side dishes are the surrounding villages, and the whole thing is an offering of gratitude. The instructor did not just show me how to shape the rice. She told me why the cone points upward, why the turmeric is non-negotiable, and why the anchovies and eggs and tempeh are arranged in a specific order around the base. By the time we sat down to eat, I understood the dish as a philosophy, not a recip.

The morning cost me around IDR 400,000 for the full experience (market walk, cooking, and meal). That is roughly USD 25. For that price you get a proper Javanese meal you cooked yourself, the market education, and a cultural context no restaurant can offer. Who is it not for? If you want a hands-off buffet or a purely Instagram-friendly plating session, this is not that. You will chop, grind, and stir. Your hands will smell of chilli for the rest of the day. That is the point.

The Moments That Made temple tours in Yogyakarta, Indonesia Worth the Trip

: the temples are still the reason I wake up at 3:30 AM. But the cooking class taught me something about them too. The same precision that built Borobudur's two million stones without mortar is the precision that a Javanese cook brings to grinding a spice paste. The same philosophy of balance that shapes a tumpeng cone is the philosophy carved into the Ramayana reliefs at Prambanan. The food and the stone come from the same culture. Understanding one helps you see the other more clearly.

I have a habit of stopping the car on the road between Mendut and Borobudur to show guests how the three temples fall on an almost perfect straight line. A sceptical traveller once said it was probably coincidence. I told him the people who hauled two million stones without mortar did not do much by accident. His whole posture toward the day changed. He started asking what else was deliberate. The cooking class does the same thing for food: it turns passive eating into active seeing.

If you are pairing the cooking class with temple visits, here is the honest logistics. Book the cooking class on a morning when you have nothing else planned. It runs about three to four hours including the market. Do not try to squeeze it in before a 4:30 AM Borobudur sunrise pickup. That is a recipe for exhaustion, not insight. Do it on a free day or on the afternoon you arrive, when your brain is still open and your stomach is empty.

Yogyakarta Cooking Class and Market Tour: A Lesser-Known Tour Worth Discovering

This specific tour has a 4.96 rating from 103 reviews on Viator, and I rarely trust numbers that high. But in this case the rating is earned. The tour is run by a small local outfit that actually lives in the village where the cooking takes place. The guide who walked me through Beringharjo had been shopping at that market since she was a child. She knew the vendors by name. She knew which stall had the freshest daun salam and which one would try to sell you last week's chillies at a premium. That local knowledge is the difference between a tour and a transaction.

The class teaches you to make tumpeng, but the real lesson is the context. Why the cone is yellow (turmeric symbolises purity and is an offering to the divine). Why the side dishes are arranged in a circle (the community surrounds the mountain, the source of life). Why you eat with your right hand (the left hand is considered unclean in traditional Javanese etiquette). These are not things you learn from a recipe card. They are things you learn from a grandmother who has been making tumpeng for fifty years, which is who your instructor is.

Who is this not for? If you are on a tight budget and just want to eat cheap street food, skip the class and go to Wijilan gudeg street near the Kraton. A plate of gudeg costs IDR 20,000 to 40,000 and is excellent. But if you want to understand what you are eating, and you want to carry a skill home that is not just a photo of a temple, this class is worth the spend.

What Really Surprised Me About Yogyakarta, Indonesia

I grew up here. I thought I knew the city. But the cooking class surprised me in three ways.

First: how much of the cooking is philosophy. I had always thought of tumpeng as a pretty rice dish served at parties. I did not know that the cone shape is a direct reference to the mountain, and that the mountain in Javanese cosmology is the axis of the world, the place where the human and the divine meet. The same mountain that looms over my house, Merapi, is the shape on the plate. I had been looking at that volcano my whole life and never connected it to the food on my tabl.

Second: how hard the work is. Grinding fresh spices with a stone mortar and pestle for twenty minutes builds a sweat. The women who do this daily have forearm strength I will never match. I have new respect for the cooking my mother did without complaint.

Third: how much the market matters. Beringharjo is not a tourist attraction. It is a working market where real Javanese people buy their daily ingredients. Walking through it with someone who can explain what you are seeing is like walking through Borobudur with someone who can read the reliefs. Without context, it is just noise. With context, it is a story.

Rama Kusuma's Insider Tips for Getting It Right

I have guided in Yogyakarta for two decades. Here is what I know that the listings do not always tell you.

Book the cooking class for a morning, not an afternoon. The morning market is at its peak between 7 and 9 AM. The produce is fresh, the vendors are energetic, and the light is good for photos. An afternoon class means a quieter market but also older ingredients and fewer interactions.

Do not eat breakfast before the market tour. You will taste samples at the spice stalls. You will be offered fresh fruit and fried snacks. Arrive hungry.

Wear shoes you can get wet and dirty. The market floor is wet. The cooking space might be an open-air pavilion with a dirt floor. Sandals are fine. White sneakers are a mistak.

Bring cash in small denominations. The market vendors do not take cards. The cooking class itself is usually prepaid online, but you will want cash for extra purchases at the market: fresh spices to take home, a bag of kopi luwak if you find a good price, or a snack from a street vendor on the way out.

Ask your guide about the ingredients. The best guides love to talk about the how and the why. Ask why the turmeric is grated instead of ground. Ask why the coconut milk is added at the end. The answers will teach you more about Javanese cooking than any recipe ever will.

Pair the class with a visit to Kotagede. The old silver-working district is a short drive from the cooking village. I brought my eldest daughter on a cycling tour through Kotagede last year, and an elderly silversmith let her hammer a small pendant. She asked him why he still worked by hand when machines are faster, and he said a machine cannot leave a person's mark in the metal. The whole tour slowed to the pace of that one bench. The cooking class and the silver district share the same spirit: slow, intentional, handmad.

What I Wish I'd Known Before I Went

I wish I had known that the cooking class would change how I see my own city. I went in thinking it was a nice activity for tourists. I came out realising I had been eating Javanese food my whole life without understanding it. That is a humbling thing to admit after twenty years of guiding.

I wish I had known to bring a container for leftovers. The tumpeng I made was enough for four people. I ate what I could and left the rest. If I had brought a small tupperware, I would have had dinner too.

I wish I had known to ask for the recipe in writing. The instructor was happy to write down the proportions for me, but I had to ask. The tour materials do not include a printed recipe. Bring a notebook or save the notes on your phon.

I wish I had known that the class is not about perfection. My tumpeng cone was lopsided. The rice kept falling off. The instructor laughed and fixed it with her hands, and said the first one is always ugly. She was right. But it tasted good, and that is the point.

I wish I had known how to say thank you properly in Javanese. After the meal, I told the instructor "matur nuwun" and she smiled wider than I had seen all morning. A little language effort goes further than any tip.

If you are coming to Yogyakarta for the temples, do that. Climb Borobudur at dawn. Walk the Prambanan galleries. Stand in the shadow of Merapi. But take one morning for the cooking class. Let the city teach you how to eat. You will leave with more than photos. You will leave with a taste that stays in your hands.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the Yogyakarta cooking class suitable for vegetarians or vegans?

Yes. Javanese cooking uses a lot of vegetables, tofu, and tempeh. The class can adapt the tumpeng side dishes to be plant-based if you tell them in advance. The sambal and the coconut-based dishes are naturally vegan. Just confirm when you book.

Do I need to know how to cook to take this class?

No. The instructor guides you through every step, from grinding the spice paste to shaping the rice cone. Absolute beginners have taken this class and produced a meal they were proud of. The only requirement is that you are willing to get your hands messy.

How long does the cooking class and market tour take?

Plan for three to four hours total. The market walk takes about an hour, the cooking takes about two hours, and eating takes the rest. The tour starts around 8 AM and finishes before noon.

Can I combine the cooking class with a temple visit on the same day?

You can, but I do not recommend it. The cooking class is a morning activity that ends around lunchtime. If you try to rush to Borobudur or Prambanan after, you will arrive in the midday heat and the crowds. Give the class its own morning and visit the temples on a separate day.

Is the market tour wheelchair accessible?

Beringharjo market is old, crowded, and has uneven floors. Wheelchair access is very difficult. If mobility is a concern, ask the tour operator if they can arrange a market alternative or skip the market portion and go straight to the cooking villag.

What should I bring to the cooking class?

Bring cash for the market (small denominations), a container for leftovers if you want them, a notebook for the recipe, and shoes you can get dirty. Sunscreen and a hat help for the market walk. A camera is nice but keep it in a plastic bag if it rains.
