Borobudur and Prambanan in One Day: Can You Really Do Both Temples?

By Rama Kusuma, Rama Kusuma is a Javanese temple guide based in Sleman, Yogyakarta

I have guided this route, Borobudur to Prambanan in a single day, more times than I can count. My father did it for thirty years before me, and I have been at it since 2006. The short answer is yes, you can see both temples in one day. The honest answer is that you will rush at least one of them, and the question you need to answer is which one deserves to be rushed and which deserves your full attention. I have watched travellers arrive at Prambanan at 4:00 PM with sore legs and distracted eyes, having spent the morning at Borobudur and the afternoon on a jeep track. They stand in front of the Shiva temple, take a photo, and leave. I have also watched the reverse: someone who blitzed Borobudur in forty minutes to save time for Prambanan, missing the whole point of the climb.

So let me be straight with you. Borobudur is roughly 40 km northwest of Yogyakarta; Prambanan is about 17 km east. The drive between them is 1 to 1.5 hours depending on traffic. That is two to three hours of your day spent in a car, not at a temple. You can do both, but you will not do both slowly. The question is whether that trade-off is worth it for you.

Why Option A Nearly Won Me Over

After that group tour, I nearly stopped recommending the private option to my guests. The group tour was efficient, the guide was competent, and the guests left satisfied. But then I remembered the German couple I guided to Setumbu Hill in 2017. The husband asked me to name the finest temple in Java. I started toward Borobudur out of habit, then stopped and said it depends what you came to feel, Prambanan reaches up like a prayer, Borobudur asks you to walk inward. He nodded and told me he trusted me more for not selling him a winner. Travellers can smell a sales pitch; honesty about trade-offs earns the trust a superlative never will.

That exchange is why I cannot tell you that Option A is the only answer. It depends on what you want to feel. If you want to feel like you saw both temples, Option A works. If you want to feel like you experienced one of them deeply, you need the private version.

Borobudur Full Climb Up And Prambanan ALL INCLUSIVE PRIVATE Tour, The Option B Experience

This tour (4.96 stars, 165 reviews) is a private 10-hour tour with total schedule flexibility. I have run this version for couples, families, and solo travellers who want to set their own pace. The key difference is not the destinations, it is the permission to stop.

I took a family from Australia on this tour last year. We started later, 6:00 AM pickup, because the father wanted to sleep in. We drove to Borobudur and I got them onto the terraces by 7:30. The daughter, maybe twelve, was fascinated by the reliefs of ships and trade routes. We spent 45 minutes on one gallery alone. I told her the story of the panel I read wrong for years, the shipwreck I had misidentified for nearly a decade until a visiting archaeologist corrected me. I thanked her in front of my group and corrected my script that afternoon. My father always said a guide who can't say 'I was wrong' is selling, not teaching. That moment, the family saw a guide being honest, not a script being recited.

We left Borobudur around 10:00, stopped for a proper lunch at a warung near Muntilan, not a tourist restaurant, and arrived at Prambanan around 1:00 PM. I took them straight to the Sewu complex first, as I always do. The tour buses were still crowding the main Shiva temple. We had Sewu almost to ourselves for 30 minutes. Then we circled back to the main Prambanan complex, and the crowds had thinned. The father said later that the Sewu-first strategy was the highlight of his day, he would never have known to do that on his own.

The Moment I Made My Decision

I have guided this route long enough to know that the decision between these two tours comes down to one question: Do you want to see both temples, or do you want to experience one of them deeply?

If you want to see both, the group tour (214335P5) is the right call. It is efficient, well-reviewed, and includes Mendut, which many travellers miss. But you will rush. You will not have time to sit on the eastern stairway of Borobudur's third terrace and watch the mist lift off the Kedu plain. You will not have time to walk to Sewu first and then return to the main Prambanan complex when the crowds have thinned.

If you want to experience one temple deeply, choose the private tour (356790P2) and accept that you will spend less time at the other. I have done this with guests who wanted to spend two hours at Prambanan's Ramayana reliefs, tracing the story of Rama and Sita carved into the stone. They left Borobudur earlier in the day, but they left Prambanan with a genuine understanding of the temple, not just a photo.

There is also a third option, the Borobudur Sunrise from Setumbu Hill, Merapi Volcano & Prambanan Full Day Tour (4.91 stars, 1,082 reviews), which bundles the three headline sights with a Setumbu sunrise. I have taken this tour with guests, and I will be honest: it is a 12-hour march. You see the misty silhouette of Borobudur from Setumbu Hill at dawn, then drive to the temple itself, then head to Merapi for the jeep tour, then finish at Prambanan. It is doable, but travellers underestimate the driving between them and end up rushing the temple they came for. I have watched too many guests arrive at Prambanan at 4:00 PM with legs coated in volcanic dust and eyes fixed on the exit. This tour is for the traveller who wants to say they did it all, not the traveller who wants to feel it.

What I Wish I'd Known Before I Went

I have been guiding this route for nearly two decades, and I still see travellers make the same mistakes. Here is what I wish every visitor knew before they set out.

Book your Borobudur climb ticket online, days ahead. Since 2023, you cannot simply walk up to the gate and climb the terraces. The structure-climb is a capped timed slot, and the daily quota is small. I have watched too many travellers learn the word 'sold out' the hard way. The foreigner courtyard ticket is around IDR 375,000 (~USD 23), and the climb-up ticket is around IDR 455,000 (~USD 28). If climbing to the top is your goal, book it online before you arrive. If you only get the courtyard ticket, the view from the lawn is still worth the dawn, but know which one you are buying.

Setumbu Hill and Borobudur are two separate paid sites. Many travellers think the hill photo IS Borobudur access. They pay the IDR 60,000 foreigner fee for Setumbu Hill, watch the sunrise, then are surprised there is a second, larger ticket for the temple itself a few kilometres away. Plan and pay for both if you want both.

Buy the combined Borobudur + Prambanan ticket. If you are doing both temples within 48 hours, the combined ticket is cheaper than two separate foreigner tickets. The operators rarely volunteer this information. At current rates, the combined ticket saves you roughly IDR 150,000 (~USD 9), enough for a good meal.

At Prambanan, walk to Sewu temple first. I cannot stress this enough. The tour buses arrive at the main Shiva temple complex starting around 9:00 AM and it gets crowded fast. Walk north to the Sewu Buddhist complex first, it is about 800 metres from the main entrance, and you will have it almost to yourself. return to the main Prambanan complex an hour later, and the crowds will have thinned. I have refined this strategy over years of guiding, and it makes a real difference in your experienc

The Ramayana Ballet's open-air theatre only runs in the dry season. If your dream is watching the ballet with the lit Prambanan temple as a backdrop, check the season. In the wet months (November–March), the show moves indoors to the covered theatre. It is still beautiful, I learned to stop apologising for the indoor stage after a honeymoon couple told me they would have missed all the dancers' faces from the outdoor seats, but if the outdoor stage is your reason to go, confirm the venue before booking.

Carry cash in small notes. At the temples, the warungs, the jeep crews, and the parking men, cards do not work. The nearest reliable ATM can be an hour back toward the city. I have seen travellers stranded at Pindul Cave with no cash and no way to pay the guide. Do not be that person.

Dry season (May–September) is your best window. The mornings are clear, the sunrises are reliable, and the Jomblang light beam is more likely to show. In the wet season (November–March), afternoon storms are common and the Prambanan open-air ballet moves indoors. Shoulder months (April, October) are transitional, morning tours usually work, but flexibility helps.

Finally, accept the trade-off. You are reading this because you want to see both Borobudur and Prambanan in one day. You can do it. But you will not do both slowly. I have watched too many travellers try to cram Borobudur, Merapi, and Prambanan into a single day and end up rushing the temple they came for. If you want to feel a temple, really feel it, the way my father taught me to feel it when he put my hand on the Karmawibhangga stone, give yourself permission to skip one thing. The temples have waited a thousand years. They will wait another day.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you really see Borobudur and Prambanan in one day?

Yes, but you will rush at least one of them. The drive between the two temples is 1 to 1.5 hours each way, so you lose 2 to 3 hours of your day in a car. A well-organised tour can show you both, but you will not have time to linger at either one. If you want to experience one temple deeply, consider a private tour that lets you control the pac

Which temple should I visit first, Borobudur or Prambanan?

Borobudur first, for the morning light. The temple faces east, and the dawn light hitting the stupas on the upper terraces is worth the early start. Prambanan in the afternoon works well because the sun lights the main Shiva temple from the west. If you reverse the order, you will be at Borobudur in harsh midday light and Prambanan in the morning shadow.

How much does it cost to visit both temples?

Foreigner entry for Borobudur courtyard is around IDR 375,000 (~USD 23), and the climb-up ticket is around IDR 455,000 (~USD 28). Prambanan foreigner entry is around IDR 375,000 (~USD 23). A combined ticket for both temples is cheaper than two separate tickets, ask at the counter or book online. The combined ticket saves you roughly IDR 150,000 (~USD 9).

Do I need to book the Borobudur climb in advance?

Yes, absolutely. Since 2023, the structure-climb is a capped timed slot with a small daily quota. You cannot simply walk up to the gate and expect to climb. Book online days ahead, especially in the dry season. If the climb slot is sold out, you can still enter the courtyard for IDR 375,000, but you will only see the temple from the lawn.

Is the Merapi jeep tour worth adding to a temple day?

Only if you have a full 12 hours and accept that you will rush Prambanan. The Borobudur Sunrise from Setumbu Hill, Merapi Volcano & Prambanan Full Day Tour is doable but it is a long day, you will arrive at Prambanan with volcanic dust on your clothes and tired legs. If the temples are your priority, skip the jeep tour and give yourself more time at the stones.

What is the best time of year to visit both temples in one day?

Dry season (May–September). The mornings are clear, the sunrises are reliable, and the afternoon heat at Prambanan is manageable. In the wet season (November–March), afternoon storms are common and can cut your time at Prambanan short. Shoulder months (April, October) are transitional, morning tours usually work, but check the forecast before booking.
